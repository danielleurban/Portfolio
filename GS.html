Generation Serve Website Redesign
About the Project
Background

Generation Serve’s mission is to develop generations of community-minded leaders. They do this through family volunteering activities and programs such as service learning and teen leadership.

Our task as a group of four bootcamp students was to discover current problems with the website and create a redesign that met both user and business needs.

  • Timeline

    3 Weeks

  • Role

    UX Designer

    UI Designer: Calendar Interface

  • Tools

    Miro, Figma

Problem

Generation Serve is an events based organization. COVID-19 caused them to lose 50% of their monthly recurring donations and more than 50% of their staff.

Solution

Our redesign will increase user engagement, streamline processes, and create clear call to action statements to increase donations.

Hypothesis

If we improved the site navigation, and expanded the home page and Activities Calendar interfaces to include the most relevant information, as well as streamlining the registration process, it would lead to an increase in registrants, volunteers, and donors.

User Interview & Survey Insights

Each team member conducted one user interview, and then we came together to discover commonalities between the diverse group of parents we talked to and create an affinity diagram. We used our insights to then produce an empathy map and user persona.

Our Research did Three Key Things

First, it validated our assumption that parents want to volunteer with their kids. We found that 81% of parents we talked to and surveyed valued volunteering with their kids.

Second, it showed us parents priorities when looking for somewhere to volunteer. These include transparency, organization, and an impact on community.

Third, it uncovered key pain points parents experience when trying to volunteer with their kids. This validated the value of what Generation Serve provides through their family volunteering calendar and gave us insight into what to prioritize in our redesign.

Usability Testing Plan

After understanding the needs of our users and stakeholders, we created a usability testing plan and conducted 4 tests on mobile and 4 on desktop.

We then narrowed down our focus using the “I like, I wish, I wonder” framework and created a feature prioritization matrix to get our priorties straight moving foward.

Feature Prioritization

After going through the feature prioritization exercise, we came up with three main areas of the site to focus on.

1

Home Page

How might we include the most relevant information on the home page and tell a story about the value of Generation Serve?

2

Registration Process

How might we streamline the registration process to keep users engaged?

3

Calendar Interface

How might we make the mobile calendar more user-friendly by adding the ability to filter events and creating hieracrchy in the design?

Develop & Test

We divided up our focus areas as a team, and I took on being the lead designer for the calendar interface. Below showcases some of my iterations for our redesign based on testing at each stage.

Testing Round 1: Current Site

Insights

01

Users asked about a way to search for events

Therefore I added filters for location, age range, and activity type.

02

Users were uncertain upon scanning which events were full and which had openings

Therefore I brainstormed ways to make the interface more scannnable using padding, spacing, and color choice.

Testing Round 2: Sketched Ideas

Insights

01

Users liked that the dates were prominent

02

It was unclear if the category tags were meant to be buttons or just displaying info

Therefore I put all info into a list with icons instead of using tags

03

Users were unsure which area was clickable to get to the event page

Therefore I thought about ways to bring in a clear Call to Action

Testing Round 3: Mid Fidelity

Insights

01

Users found the touch targets too small for the dropdowns

Therefore I dug into accesibility standards and enlarged the buttons

02

"The filter button feels unnecessary, the drop down should automatically filter"

03

Users responded well to the event titles

This validated my assumption that tiles would make scanning easier

Testing Round 4: High Fidelity

Insights
01

Users responded well to the date filter

This helped us with our goal of cutting down information overload and decreasing time on task.

02

New layout made drop downs more usable and accesibile.

Translating Mobile First Design to Desktop

When branstorming sketches and looking at competitors for UI inspiration, I had a hard time conceptualizing how to fit important information while not overwhelming the user. After deliberation as a team, we decided to have two views available for the desktop version. The "List View," as well as a "Calendar View," with a toggle to switch back and forth.

"Calendar View" Mid-Fi

Testing Insights

01

0 % of users noticed the toggle to change from Calendar to list view

Therefore I changed the toggle to a more prominent spot, enlarging it, and labeling the icons.

02

Users responded well to only being shown details for open events

This validated my assumption that not including details for full events cut down on information overload.

"List View" Mid-Fi

Testing Insights

01

Users found tiles too wide to quickly scan

Therefore I iterated to create two columns of tiles for desktop.

02

Users were less likely to click into event on desktop as compared to mobile at this stage

Therefore I added a clear call to action to mirror the mobile design better

"Calendar View" High-Fi

Testing Insights

01

Users closed out of the floating Call to Action before reading it

Therefore we moved it to a fixed position under the Calendar

02

80% of users preferred the Calendar view

Therefore we made it the default

"List View" High-Fi

Testing Insights

01

The current re-design made users find the event they were looking for 90% faster on desktop.

02

Some users expressed that they liked the details available on the list view.

Retrospective

I learned so much through the course of this project about working on a team with other designers. By taking the lead on one of the website's key features, I learned to trust my own ideas and the importance of requesting feedback early and often from my team.

By going through several rounds of testing I learned that while trusting my ideas is imporant initially, I need to always be open to changing and adapting them based on data.
